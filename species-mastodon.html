Natural history

A browser,
not a grazer

The American mastodon is frequently confused with the woolly mammoth, but the two were evolutionary cousins separated by millions of years. Where mammoths were open grassland grazers, mastodons were forest browsers — adapted to the dense spruce and pine forests of Pleistocene North America, feeding on branches, twigs, and conifers.

Mastodons ranged across virtually the entire North American continent, from Alaska and Canada down through the United States into Central America. Their teeth — large, cone-shaped cusps designed for crushing wood and vegetation — distinguish them clearly from the ridged grinding teeth of mammoths.

Males grew tusks up to 2.5 meters in length, used in combat with rivals and foraging through deep snow. Their social structure likely resembled modern elephants: matriarchal family groups, extended memory, complex communication.

"The mastodon's teeth tell a story of forests — they were built to crush branches, not graze grass."

Fossil evidence shows that mastodons co-existed with at least five other species of megafauna in Pleistocene North America — horses, camels, giant ground sloths, and short-faced bears — forming a rich, interconnected ecosystem that has no modern parallel.

Stomach contents preserved in some specimens have revealed their diet in remarkable detail: spruce twigs, pine cones, mosses, and the bark of birch and alder. Some individuals were preserved with enough soft tissue to identify muscle structure and fat deposits, providing a vivid picture of these animals in life.

Extinction

The thousand-year collapse

The American mastodon survived at least three major glacial cycles over its three-million-year history — climate change was not novel to this species. What was novel was us.

The timing of mastodon extinction correlates precisely with the arrival of Clovis people in North America approximately 13,000 years ago. Within roughly a thousand years of human arrival, mastodons — along with roughly 35 other large mammal species — had vanished from the continent.

This is the essence of the "overkill hypothesis," most famously championed by paleoecologist Paul S. Martin: human hunters, encountering prey with no evolutionary experience of bipedal predators with projectile weapons, could have driven these animals to extinction with remarkable speed.

The mastodon's relatively slow reproductive rate — likely similar to modern elephants, with a 22-month gestation and years between calves — would have made recovery from even modest hunting pressure nearly impossible.

Clovis and pre-Clovis peoples enter North America via Beringia. Archaeological sites show unmistakable evidence of mastodon butchering.
~10,000 BP
Extinction
The last mastodon populations disappear. Radiocarbon dates suggest a rapid, synchronous collapse across the continent — not a slow, climate-driven decline.
Factor 01
Overkill Hunting
Clovis spear points found in direct association with mastodon remains at multiple sites across North America provide direct evidence of hunting. Mastodons had never encountered human hunters and likely showed little fear response.
Factor 02
Climate Stress
End-Pleistocene warming was reshaping North American forests, fragmenting mastodon habitat. Climate stress alone may not have caused extinction, but it reduced population resilience — leaving animals more vulnerable to hunting pressure.
Factor 03
Reproductive Vulnerability
With long gestation periods and slow reproductive rates, mastodon populations could not recover from even moderate human predation. Models show that hunting as few as 1–2 animals per group per year could collapse a regional population within centuries.
Genomics research

What the genome
tells us

The mastodon genome has been sequenced from multiple specimens, with the highest-quality data coming from specimens preserved in permafrost and peat bogs across North America. The nuclear genome has been published and is publicly available in NCBI.

Crucially, the mastodon genome has clarified a long-standing evolutionary debate: while mastodons and mammoths are both proboscideans (the order that includes modern elephants), they diverged approximately 25–28 million years ago — far more distantly related than the woolly mammoth and modern Asian elephant.

This makes the mastodon a different kind of de-extinction challenge. Unlike the mammoth, where relatively few gene edits to Asian elephant cells might reconstitute key phenotypic traits, mastodon resurrection would require far more extensive genomic engineering — or identifying a closer proxy species.

Research program: Mastodon

Rewild Genomics is building bioinformatic pipelines for comparative analysis of the mastodon nuclear genome against living proboscidean references, with a focus on identifying the genetic architecture of cold-adaptation traits, dietary specialization loci, and immune system divergence.

Our primary research goal is developing open-source tools for analyzing the genomic basis of mastodon-specific phenotypic traits — contributing to the foundational data infrastructure that any future de-extinction program would require.
