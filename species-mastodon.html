Natural history

A browser,
not a grazer

The American mastodon is often confused with the woolly mammoth, but the two were only distantly related. Mammoths were grassland grazers; mastodons lived in forests, moving through the dense spruce and pine stands of Pleistocene North America and feeding on branches, twigs, and conifers.

They ranged across most of North America, from Alaska south into Central America. Their teeth are the clearest way to tell them apart from mammoths: large, cone-shaped cusps built for crushing wood and vegetation, rather than the ridged grinding surfaces mammoths used on grass.

Males grew tusks up to 2.5 meters long, used for fighting rivals and pushing through snow. Their social structure probably resembled modern elephants, with matriarchal family groups, long memories, and complex communication.

"Mastodon teeth are built for crushing branches. Everything about them says forest, not grassland."

Fossil evidence shows mastodons sharing North America with at least five other megafauna species: horses, camels, giant ground sloths, short-faced bears. It was a richness of large animals the continent has not seen since.

Some specimens have preserved stomach contents: spruce twigs, pine cones, moss, birch and alder bark. A few were preserved with enough soft tissue to show muscle structure and fat deposits, giving a more detailed picture of these animals than bones alone ever could.

Extinction

The thousand-year collapse

The American mastodon had already lived through at least three major glacial cycles over its three-million-year history. Climate change was nothing new to this species. People were.

Mastodon extinction lines up closely with the arrival of Clovis people in North America around 13,000 years ago. Within roughly a thousand years, mastodons and about 35 other large mammal species were gone from the continent.

This is the core of the overkill hypothesis, championed by paleoecologist Paul S. Martin: human hunters encountering prey that had no experience of bipedal predators with projectile weapons could drive them to extinction faster than most people assume possible.

Mastodons likely reproduced at roughly the same rate as modern elephants, with a 22-month gestation and years between calves. That pace makes recovery from even modest hunting pressure nearly impossible.

Mastodons survive the coldest period of the last ice age, retreating south and then expanding again as the climate begins to warm.

First humans arrive
Clovis and pre-Clovis peoples enter North America via Beringia. Archaeological sites show unmistakable evidence of mastodon butchering.
~10,000 BP
Extinction
The last mastodon populations disappear. Radiocarbon dates point to a rapid collapse across the continent, not a slow climate-driven retreat.
Factor 01
Overkill Hunting
Clovis spear points found in direct association with mastodon remains at multiple sites across North America provide direct evidence of hunting. Mastodons had never encountered human hunters and likely showed little fear response.
Factor 02
Climate Stress
End-Pleistocene warming was reshaping North American forests and fragmenting mastodon habitat. Climate stress alone probably was not enough to cause extinction, but it reduced population resilience and likely made animals more vulnerable to hunters.
Factor 03
Reproductive Vulnerability
With long gestation periods and slow reproductive rates, mastodon populations could not recover from even moderate human predation. Models show that hunting as few as 1–2 animals per group per year could collapse a regional population within centuries.
Genomics research

What the genome
tells us

The mastodon genome has been sequenced from multiple specimens, with the highest-quality data coming from specimens preserved in permafrost and peat bogs across North America. The nuclear genome has been published and is publicly available in NCBI.

The mastodon genome has settled a long-standing debate: though mastodons and mammoths are both proboscideans (the order that includes modern elephants), they diverged roughly 25 to 28 million years ago, making them far more distantly related than the woolly mammoth and the modern Asian elephant.

That distance makes mastodon de-extinction a fundamentally different problem. With mammoths, a relatively small number of edits to Asian elephant cells could recover key traits. With mastodons, the gap is much wider, and any viable path forward would require either far more extensive genomic work or finding a closer living proxy species.

Research program: Mastodon

Rewild Genomics is building bioinformatic pipelines for comparative analysis of the mastodon nuclear genome against living proboscidean references, with a focus on identifying the genetic architecture of cold-adaptation traits, dietary specialization loci, and immune system divergence.

Our primary research goal is developing open-source tools for analyzing the genomic basis of mastodon-specific traits, contributing to the data infrastructure any future de-extinction program would need to build on.
