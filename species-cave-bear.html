The largest bear to ever walk the Earth lived not in open wilderness but deep in cave systems across Europe — sacred enough to ancient humans that their skulls were arranged in ritual formations. Then, at the peak of the last Ice Age, they were gone.

Natural history

Despite its fearsome name and size, the cave bear was predominantly herbivorous — its teeth and jaw structure strongly suggest a diet of plants, berries, roots, and possibly honey, more similar to a giant panda than to the brown bear it resembled externally. The "cave" in its name comes not from predatory hunting behavior but from its extraordinary use of limestone cave systems for hibernation.

Cave bears hibernated in enormous numbers in the same cave systems year after year, generation after generation. The floors of caves across Europe — particularly in the Alps, the Carpathians, and the Caucasus — are carpeted with their bones: at Drachenhöhle in Austria alone, the remains of over 30,000 individual bears have been found.

These cave systems became, in a sense, the cave bear's great vulnerability. When Neanderthals and then anatomically modern humans began competing for the same sheltered spaces — for fire, for storage, for winter refuge — they were directly displacing the cave bear from the habitat it depended on for survival.

"Cave bears hibernated in such numbers that their bones form the literal floors of Alpine cave systems."

The cave bear holds a unique place in the archaeology of early human ritual. At the Neanderthal site of Bruniquel Cave in France, cave bear skulls were arranged in deliberate patterns alongside stone structures — one of the earliest known examples of symbolic human behavior, dating to approximately 176,000 years ago.

This means Neanderthals and cave bears shared Europe for hundreds of thousands of years — a coexistence that somehow worked until modern humans arrived and changed the competitive dynamics entirely.

Extinction

Squeezed out
from every side

The cave bear's extinction, finalized around 24,000 years ago at the peak of the Last Glacial Maximum, is a case study in how multiple pressures can collapse even a large, successful species when they arrive simultaneously.

Genomic studies have revealed a gradual population decline beginning around 50,000 years ago — coinciding with the arrival of anatomically modern humans in Europe. This was not a sudden crash but a slow compression over tens of thousands of years as the bears were progressively pushed out of their cave systems and their forest habitats fragmented under glacial cooling.

The bear's specialized dependence on caves for hibernation was its critical vulnerability. Modern humans, arriving with fire and advanced tool technology, had strong incentives to claim the same limestone cave systems — and they did so systematically. Cave bear remains in human-occupied sites show consistent evidence of hunting and butchering; the bears were killed for meat, fat, and fur.

By the time the Last Glacial Maximum hit — the coldest period of the last ice age, around 26,000–20,000 BP — the cave bear had lost most of its range, most of its hibernation habitat, and much of its genetic diversity. The final populations were too small and too fragmented to recover.

Factor 01
Cave Competition
Human occupation of limestone cave systems directly displaced cave bears from their essential hibernation habitat. Unlike brown bears, which can den in excavated burrows or dense vegetation, cave bears were highly dependent on specific cave structures.
Factor 02
Direct Hunting
Butchering marks on cave bear bones at multiple European archaeological sites confirm systematic hunting. Cave bear fur, fat, and meat were valuable resources to Paleolithic humans navigating harsh glacial winters.
Factor 03
Habitat Fragmentation
The Last Glacial Maximum compressed European forests into isolated refugia, fragmenting cave bear populations. Genomic analysis reveals progressive loss of genetic diversity during this period, leaving remaining populations too small to sustain themselves.
Genomics research

A Middle Pleistocene
genome calibrated

The cave bear has exceptional genomic coverage compared to most extinct species. Because cave systems act as natural bone preservation chambers — stable temperature, humidity, and chemistry — cave bear specimens from across Europe have yielded DNA ranging from 24,000 to over 300,000 years old. Multiple nuclear genomes have been published, along with extensive mitochondrial datasets.

A landmark 2020 study published in Current Biology produced a Middle Pleistocene cave bear genome — one of the oldest near-complete nuclear genomes of any extinct species — allowing researchers to calibrate the evolutionary history of bears across over a million years.

The cave bear and modern brown bear are more closely related than either is to the American black bear — their genomes show extensive ancient hybridization, with evidence of gene flow between the two species occurring even as cave bear populations were declining. The brown bear is therefore a strong candidate proxy species for any future de-extinction work.

Research program: Cave Bear

Rewild Genomics is building alignment and comparative analysis pipelines for cave bear genomic data, with particular focus on the herbivory-associated gene clusters that distinguish cave bears from omnivorous brown bears — a rare case of a large carnivoran evolving toward herbivory, with implications for understanding dietary adaptation in mammals broadly.

The cave bear's deep genomic record also makes it valuable as a temporal calibration species for testing our aDNA damage-correction and assembly tools across a range of specimen ages.
